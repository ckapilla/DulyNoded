  • What does "Halving Cycle" Even Mean?

    The only real pre-requisite to benefit from this paper is having some knowledge that the issuance of bitcoins takes place on a fixed schedule that cannot be modified. This schedule cuts the rate of issuance in half every four years, thus the term 'Halving Cycle'. It briefest summary, it looks like this:

    There is a lot of information in this chart, but by the time we are finished it will make perfect sense to you.

    This depicts the two mirror image consequences of Bitcoin's Halvings â€” the increasing number of coins mined 'so far' which increases logarithmically (meaning it gets closer and closer to its terminal value of 21,000,000 but never actually reaches it), and the ever-decreasing number of coins remaining to be mined, which decreases logarithmically (meaning it gets close and closer to its terminal value of zero, but never actually reaches it.)

    Note that although the first number is increasing and the second number is decreasing, both numbers equally embody halving: the rate of change for both is halved every four years. That is:

    1. For every four year cycle, the Total Number of Coins remaining to be produced in the future (out of the original. fixed total of 21 million) is reduced by one half, specifically the coins are reduced by:10.5 million, 5.25 million, 2.625 million, 1.3125 million, etc. | In percentage terms, for each four year cycle the percent of total coins remaining is 50%, 25%, 12.5%, 6.25%, etc. of the original Total Number of Coins.
    1. For every four year cycle, the Increase in Total Number of Coins that have been mined is reduced by one half, specifically the number of coins mined are: 10.5 million, 5.25 million, 2.625 million,1.3125 million, etc. In percentage terms, for each four year cycle the percent of increase total coins already mined is 50%, 25%, 12.5%, 6.25%, etc. of the original Total Number of Coins.

    For every four year cycle, the block reward earned for each 10 minute period, i.e., the actual Number of Coins that a 'lucky' miner earns for creating a new 'block' is reduced by a half compared to the previous cycle. (The block rewards are 50, 25, 12.5, 6.25, etc.)

    Take a minute to consider this â€” the actual number of coins produced during each 10 minute period (#3 above) has the same "number" (uses the same numerals) as the Percentage difference in #'s 1 and 2 above. There is no reason for this! There is no reason, other than mathematical elegance, why these numbers should be the same. There is simply no connection between the absolute number '50' and the percentage '50%'. Why are they the same? Because Satoshi said so. Why? Because using these same numbers makes the math behind Bitcoin all the more elegant, more simple, more beautiful.

    We can see this graphically in the following chart, which maps 3 sets of data, but only two are visible. Note especially the two different Y-axes â€” one is absolute number of coins over the entire 140 year lifespan of Bitcoins issuance cycles, and the other is the block reward in each 10 minute block.

  • Could we have had 'Thirding Cycles' rather than Halving Cycles?

    In the context of Bitcoin, it is crucial that our chosen formula has a limit and does not get continue to get larger as the number of steps increases. (As for example 1/211/2^11/21ï»¿ does.)

    But are there other geometric formulas that would work just as well? We should consider for completeness some other options â€” for example

    1/3+1/9+1/27+...or:1/321/3 + 1/9 + 1/27 + ... or :1/3^21/3+1/9+1/27+...or:1/32ï»¿

    Here each term is multiplied by 1/3 instead of 1/2. So we are 'thirding' rather that 'halving'. We could also consider multiplying by 1/4, but 'quartering' has some rather unpleasant medieval connotations! In addition 'To Quarter' something really does not fit the the process of minting coins. (And 'To Third' is not even recognizable as a verb.)

    With the halving process we start with a complete 'universe' of coins to be minted eventually (analogous to the complete square in the above illustration), and with every period we take the coins remaining to be produced and subtract out half of that number to be produced during this period. So at every step we have two relevant numbers â€” total mined and total yet-to-be mined, and eventually using up (virtually) the entire square.

    If we were to try to do quartering instead... well it just wouldn't work â€” as it implies dividing something into four equal parts, but we aren't dealing with four parts here, only two â€” total mined and total remaining. Don't forget the big picture here is that we are needing to get coins created and distributed over time. No matter what, after each step we need the two values total mined and total-remaining. The Halving process, and only a Halving process fits the needs of this scenario perfectly; any other possible fractions don't fit it at all.
